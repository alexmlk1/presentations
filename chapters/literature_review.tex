\section*{Unlearning or Untraining?}
The goal of this section is to compare the discussion above with the thoughts expressed in \cite{untraining_2026}.\\
In the introduction, the authors makes a remark that was common with N.P.: there is a lot of different tasks that the literature refers to as "unlearning", which, conceptually, are different from merely "deleting data from the model". 
Some examples are: removing dangerous knowledge, eliminating backdoor or poisoning attacks, unlearn copyrighted content or specific art styles, etc.\\
The main point they rise is that this class of problems is too broad to be faced in the same way, and thus the need to break down the "unlearning" concept into two different ones: UNTRAINING and UNLEARNING.\\
UNTRAINING is the task of removing the influence of a certain data point from the model, without changing its behaviour on the rest of the dataset (the gold-standard in this case is retraining).\\
On the other hand, UNLEARNING aims at "removing the influence of the underlying distribution from which those examples are sampled".

As far as the measures are concerned, in the former case, the $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-certified removal is the reference measure, but it does not capture the desired effect in the latter case.
A general idea that arises from the paper is the fact that untraining is a point-wise concept, while unlearning is at distribution level. 
Therefore, to compare the two concepts, we need to get them at the same level in the first place.
This can be done by saying that UNLEARNING "aims to approximate a model that was never trained on any instance of that distribution".

An implication of the previously expressed discrepancies is that successful untraining does not imply misclassifying the examples in the forget set (because if someone else took a picture of the tour eiffel, then removing you has no effect on the prediction), nor the necessity of changning the model parameters, whereas the unlearning implies both.\\
If we want to give a definition similar of unlearning in terms of certified removal, we could proceed in the following way.\\
Given a concept $\mathcal{B}$ we want to unlearn, let $\mathcal{S}^{\mathrm{full}}$ be the complete set of examples that capture the behaviour $\mathcal{B}$.
Unlearning $\mathcal{S}\sim\mathcal{B}$ is equivalent to untraining on $\mathcal{S}^{\mathrm{full}}$.
Remarkably, if $\mathcal{S}=\mathcal{S}^{\mathrm{full}}$, then untraining $\mathcal{S}$ and unlearning $\mathcal{B}$ are the same.

This formulation is very clear to me, but I'm not sure if the independence condition previously stated in Attempt 2 would be equivalent.
If they are, assuming that the concept of behaviour coincide with the distribution of a user, the definition given from attempt 2 is close to their definition of un\-learning.
Note, however, that I would like the predictor to not be able to see what user the data comes from. 
If that's not the case, one can keep some information about the unlearnt class that can give room to attacks that try to recognize if an element was part of the class to forget or not.

Althought they clearly say that their aim is not to discuss the feasibility of unlearning, I think that there are some examples in the literature of unlearning algorithms in this new sense, such as SurgUN \cite{competition_forgetting_2026} or DIET \cite{diet_2026} (as well as the one by Li et al. discussed in the paper).
The first one unlearns concepts by changing the model parameters after training on concurrent images, while the second one moves the embedding of the concept towards the boundary of the elliptic space of embeddings, making its influence on the prediction negligible.

\paragraph{Why does this matter?} The lack of clear specification of problem settings can leave us with unclear expectations for when an algorithm should work well.
  The two problem settings of Untraining and Unlearning are fundamentally distinct, and we may falsely expect an algorithm that performs well for one to also work well for the other, but, since the evaluation metrics are different, this may not be the case.
  For example, in the case study of unlearning corrupted data, mere untraining on $\mathcal{S}\subset \mathcal{S}^{\mathrm{full}}$ fails catastrophically to heal the model, whereas unlearning the behaviour is what we would need (refer to \cite{schoepf_redirection_2026} to see an attempt of UNLEARNING algorithm in this case).

\section*{Algorithm implementations: D2D vs R2D}
An application for which we may want to use influence functions is unlearning a data point (for example for privacy reasons).
However, as we have seen, IFs may not work well in the MLP and Deep Learning setting. 
Therefore, in the literature some algorithms have been developed to unlearn training points without using the highly inefficient LOO retraining.
For example, in \cite{mu_rewind--delete_2025}, the authors take inspiration from the \emph{Descend-to-Delete} algorithm (D2D) and create a more efficient version called \emph{Rewind-to-Delete} (R2D).

The former algorithm is very intuitive and consists on simply continuing the training of the model but on the updated dataset where we deleted the target data point.
By continuing for enough iteration, there have been proven some theoretical guarantees that the final parameter distribution is in some sense undistinguishable from the one of LOO method.\\
The problem with this algorithm is that such guarantees only hold in the convex case and cannot be extended to the more general setting (e.g. ReLU and tanh functions are not convex, so this result does not hold).

On the other hand, the R2D algorithm saves a check-point parameter during the training (let's say after the K-th iteration) and then keeps going until iteration T.
The idea is that when we receive the request of unlearning the target point, we will continue the training on the updated dataset starting from $\theta(K)$, instead of $\theta(T)$.\\
The authors of the paper show that also this method yields indistinguishalbility, but in this case the result holds also for non-convex functions and the iterations required are much less than the total training time $T$ (required by LOO).

In \cite{mu_SGD_2025}, the same researchers extend the previous results also to the (projected) SGD case.
In particular, in addition to all previous observations, they highlight that D2D provides tighter bounds for the undistinguishability due to its reliance on the convergence to a unique global minimum, while R2D has more loose estimates as it only counts on the underlying contractivity of gradient systems.

\section*{Other interesting things we might want to explore}
\begin{itemize}
  \item If our goal is to gain a better understanding of what data points are the most informative during the training, instead of trying to unlearn certain data, the results in \cite{ghorbani_data_2019} might be interesting.
  In their paper, the authors prove that their Shapley values-based method performs better on this task rather than influence function methods. 
  \item It may be of interest to study Bayesian Influence Functions (BIFs) as well as frequentistic ones. 
  In \cite{kreer_bayesian_2025}, the researchers present an unlearning method that uses BIFs instead of IFs. The reason for this is we don't need to compute the iHVP to evaluate BIFs, therefore this method works better with more singular loss landscapes.
  As another consequence, we don't need to evaluate these quantities on local minima (we dont need the hessian to be invertible), which is one of the less realistic hypotheses for IFs.\\
  On the other hand, computations are not always faster than IFs (here, the leading cost is estimating the covariance between two elements) and achieving good results requires more hyperparameter tuning than classical methods.
  \item Paper about machine unlearning with $\eps\neq0$ \cite{qiao_soft_2025}: they introduce a soft-weigthed framework for unlearning data with the aim of improving robustness or fairness.
  The main point is that if we are not in a privacy-related field, there is no need to completely forget a datapoint; instead, it would be more beneficial to consider it with a lower weight.
  Moreover, instead of training from scratch the model with the updated weights, the authors propose to correct the result of the usual training with influence functions.
  \item Something I had not seen yet in machine unlearning, but I have seen for example in plateau explanation \cite{Berthier_2024}: it can make more sense to consider influence functions in certain epochs of the training, not only at the end \cite{lee_timescales_2025}. 
  Indeed, the importance of a certain data point may change throughout the training.
  \item One of the reasons why we are analyzing unlearning is that it's kind of the dual problem of finiding influencial samples.    However, the authors of \cite{pan_misalignment_2026} state that in general this correlation is not high. 
  Therefore, they propose a new training method for neural nets that increases this alignment while keeping about the same performances of the usual algorithms in terms of training time and accuracy.
    
  {\color{RoyalBlue}
    \item The way we have studied influence functions for unlearning so far is to estimate a step of the Newton algorithm to approximate the effect of LOO retraining.
    In \cite{bui2026batch}, they propose an iterative algorithm using the influence function approximation step by step. The main issue they managed to solve is the computa-tional cost for such algorithm:
    computing only hessian-vector products instead of the whole hessian and using stochastic hessians as proxies for the full-batch hessian.
    \item Lately, in the psychology area, I have seen an advancement concerning also machine learning, where they state that forgetting (unlearning) is not due to time, but more to interference of new information that is very close to the old one.
    The study from \cite{barman_geometry_2026} compares human forgetting and ML forgetting and shows that competition is the main source of forgetting, both in machines and humans alike.\\
    Exploiting this phenomenon, in \cite{competition_forgetting_2026}, the authors pro\-pose a new method for unlearning data points that relies on unlearning concepts in Vision Language Models by changing a surgically chosen set of parameters, that is responsible for the prediction of the concept we want to unlearn, by updating them with gradients given by competing concepts.
    \item Maybe it's better to use continual learning for Pl@ntNet instead of retraining the model every 3 months, now that results improving perfor\-mances \cite{catastrophic_forgetting_2026} are available.  
  } 
\end{itemize}
